# Method Checkpoints

Use these checkpoints as light scaffolding. Do not let them replace the user's own reading style.

## Three-Pass Reading

Adapted from S. Keshav's "How to Read a Paper":

1. First pass: category, context, correctness, contributions, clarity.
2. Second pass: inspect figures, methods, and evidence without getting trapped in every detail.
3. Third pass: mentally reconstruct the paper and ask what experiment you would run next.

Use this when deciding whether to skim, deep-read, or rebuild the full argument.

## Active Scientific Reading

Use an active-reading loop:

- Define why the user is reading this paper.
- Predict the author's claim before checking every result.
- Read figures as the backbone.
- Summarize each section in the user's own words.
- Convert confusion into explicit `Q:` entries.
- Revisit questions after reading Discussion and Methods.

## CARS Background Model

Use the Create a Research Space pattern for introductions:

- Establish the territory: what field/process matters?
- Establish the niche: what is unknown, contradictory, or technically unresolved?
- Occupy the niche: what question/hypothesis/method does this paper use?

For journal-club slides, this usually becomes:

`现象/重要性 -> 已知机制 -> 未解决问题 -> 本文科学问题`.

## Critical Appraisal

Borrow the validity-results-applicability structure:

- Are the results valid?
- What exactly are the results?
- Are they important?
- Can they be applied to other models, diseases, or the user's project?

For mechanism papers, translate this into:

- Does the perturbation actually test the proposed mechanism?
- Are direct and indirect effects separated?
- Are orthogonal methods used?
- Are negative/positive controls convincing?
- Are cell-line findings generalized?

## Journal Club Story Arc

Use this structure for a presentation:

1. Why should the audience care?
2. What was known before this paper?
3. What exact gap remains?
4. What did the authors test?
5. How did each figure move the argument?
6. What model do the authors propose?
7. What is strong, weak, or missing?
8. What can we borrow for our own work?
